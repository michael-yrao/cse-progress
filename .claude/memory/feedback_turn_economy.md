**In interactive coaching (derive-the-design, Socratic pushback, failure-mode drills), keep each
turn to ONE job:** a one-line affirmation + at most one correction + one question. Then stop. The
back-and-forth *is* the teaching — a turn does not need to also sharpen, tabulate, and pre-empt the
next three follow-ups.

**Push depth into the note, not the chat.** Tables, mnemonics, full derivations, ACID-style
breakdowns belong in the written tech/tracker note (e.g. [redis.md]) — updated live and referenced
("added to your note"), so the chat stays conversational and the note becomes the thing they reread.
The chat is the *dialogue*; the note is the *reference*. Don't render the reference inline mid-session.

**Progressive disclosure.** Short answer first, then *offer* the deeper why ("want the tradeoff
table?") instead of dumping it unprompted. Let the learner pull detail.

**Trust the right answer.** When they nail it, acknowledge in one line and move on — never re-explain
what they just demonstrated they know.

**Why:** the Jul 15 2026 Redis derive session was pedagogically excellent and the learner loved it —
but each of my turns stacked affirm + correct + sharpen + ask + tables into walls of text. Their
words: "it helped a lot, the issue is it was a lot of walls of text." The *depth* helped; the
*packaging* buried it. Those are separable — same substance, spread across turns and into the note,
so no single message is a wall.

**How to apply:** this is [[feedback-spine-first]] extended from the opening of an explanation to the
*whole multi-turn session*, and it's how [[feedback-interactive-learning]] should actually feel on the
page. An instance of [[feedback-operating-principles]] / token discipline. Applies to all interactive
coaching (SD, AI-eng, DSA), not just Redis.
